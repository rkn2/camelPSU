\section{Introduction}

This ``transformation" track proposal will develop an effective and scalable approach to teaching EM-contextualized AI (EMxAI), with a focus on engaging undergraduate students from groups traditionally underrepresented in computing courses and careers.
As directly noted in the current IUSE CUE program solicitation, \textbf{a key challenge for computing in undergraduate education lies at the intersection of computing and other disciplines \cite{nsf24553}.}
Presently, there is a growing demand for computer science departments to teach AI material to students from all disciplines so that students can progress in AI education pathways. 
However, these pathways need to be crafted so that students are not only users of AI but also responsible creators and problematizers of the quickly shifting needs and methods of the future workforce.
The proposed work will ease that burden by facilitating the teaching of AI topics in engineering mechanics (EM) specific courses across a diverse cohort of undergraduate learners. Specifically, the key objectives are:
\begin{gitemize3}
\item Develop EMxAI resources that integrate AI concepts and skills within specific engineering mechanics domains like structural analysis, manufacturing, and energy modeling.
\end{gitemize3}

\begin{gitemize0}
\item Create an educational infrastructure (e.g. online platform) to support the sharing and dissemination of these EMxAI educational resources across institutions.
\end{gitemize0}

\begin{gitemize2}
\item Foster connected communities of engineering professors teaching and doing research on EMxAI with undergraduate students from diverse backgrounds.
\end{gitemize2}

\begin{gitemize4}
\item Establish supportive career pathways for underrepresented engineering students interested in the intersection of computing and engineering mechanics.
\end{gitemize4}

The \textbf{overarching goal is of this work} to better prepare a diverse range of engineering students to effectively use, create, and critically analyze AI applications within their EM disciplines. By contextualizing AI education within real-world engineering problems and providing inclusive learning environments, the project aims to increase engagement, retention, and career prospects for underrepresented groups in these emerging technological fields.

The Engineering Mechanics Institute (EMI) ``promotes research and the application of scientific and mathematical principles to address existing and emerging engineering and societal issues" \cite{CITE EMI WEBPAGE}.
The project will be a collaborative, nationwide effort of four EMI faculty across diverse universities to ensure that this work and its derivatives support students from groups historically underrepresented in computing. 
While the work will be piloted at only four EMI institutions, the intent of this project is to create a scaleable EMxAI ecosystem that can later be disseminated to other engineering mechanics programs.
To ensure that this ecosystem is scalable to a wide range of institutions, the project team includes a female assistant professor at Penn State, a female associate professor at Texas A\&M University, a Hispanic-serving institution (HSI), a female, Asian assistant professor at Tennessee State University, a historically Black college (HBCU), and a male, Hispanic associate professor at University of New Mexico, a HSI and First-generation Forward Institution (FGFI) with over 50\% of the population as first-generation college students \cite{Munoz2022}.

In terms of specific EM domains addressed in this pilot, PI Napolitano's research and undergraduate teaching focus on historic preservation and damage diagnostics; CoPI Paal's research and undergraduate teaching focus on risk assessment and modeling structural stability; CoPI Reinhart's research and undergraduate teaching focus on optimized design and control of additive manufacturing processes; CoPI Wang's research and undergraduate teaching focus on building energy modeling; CoPI Moreu's research and undergraduate teaching focus on structural health monitoring.

By focusing on these specific technical AI applications within engineering, our project aims to equip engineering students with the practical skills and knowledge needed to use, create, and problematize AI effectively in their future careers.

In addition to developing specific EMxAI materials, which will be discussed in more depth in Task 2, this project will take a meta-level approach to inform best practices for developing inclusive course materials and career pathways. Specifically, we will collaborate with Penn State's Division of Development and Alumni Relations (DDAR) Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) program to review and assess our educational resources. Through this partnership, we will synthesize lessons learned and formulate guidelines that can guide other educators in creating equitable and culturally responsive AI-integrated curricula.
The DDAR DEI program brings expertise in fostering inclusive learning environments and promoting diversity in higher education. By leveraging their knowledge and conducting a thorough evaluation of our developed materials, we will identify successful strategies and potential areas for improvement. By taking this meta-level approach and partnering with experts in DEI, this project will not only produce contextualized EMxAI materials but also contribute to the broader discourse on equitable AI education. The dissemination of these best practice guidelines will empower educators across institutions to develop AI-integrated curricula that foster inclusive and welcoming learning environments, ultimately broadening participation in this critical technological domain.

\section{Background and rationale}

The rapid advancement of AI is transforming numerous industries, including engineering, by enabling powerful new capabilities for design optimization, control systems, predictive maintenance, and more \cite{xxx}.
This is evidenced by statistics such as the fact that 81\% of engineering companies surveyed by the World Economic Forum said that they would be incorporating AI into their workflows by 2025 \cite{WEF2020}.
As AI becomes increasingly integrated into engineering practices, the reciprocal relationship between AI and engineering must be considered.
In their 2020 opinion piece, Johri pointed out the two way information transfer between AI and engineers.  They said that AI ``embeds the idea of how engineers, among others, see the world; these embedded ideas, in turn, shape the work in which they live"  \cite{johri2020artificial}.
This perspective highlights the importance of engineers' roles in shaping AI systems and the subsequent impact of these systems on the engineering profession and society at large.
Continuing on, Johri emphasized that this is a burden engineering educators need to be cognizant of as it is not insignificant \cite{johri2020artificial}.

These pathways need to be crafted so that students are not only users of AI but also responsive creators and problematizers of the quickly shifting needs and methods of the future workforce. 
Students must learn how to effectively leverage and apply existing AI tools and techniques within their EM domains; this involves understanding AI systems' capabilities and limitations, as well as how to interpret and utilize their outputs.
Beyond just using AI, EM students need to develop the skills to build and develop new AI models and applications tailored to the unique challenges and requirements of their field; this includes tasks such as data preprocessing, model training, and system integration. 
Perhaps most importantly, EM students must be equipped with the critical thinking skills to scrutinize and question AI systems, identifying potential biases, ethical concerns, or societal impacts. As the creators and implementers of AI in EM contexts, they will play a crucial role in shaping the responsible development and deployment of these technologies.
To prepare students to engage AI effectively in engineering careers, it is crucial that undergraduate EM programs integrate use, creation, and problematization of AI-related knowledge and skills into the curriculum \cite{xxx}. 
By emphasizing all three aspects--use, creation, and problematization--engineering programs can produce graduates who are not just passive consumers of AI, but active and informed participants in shaping the future of AI-driven engineering practices. 
This holistic approach aims to cultivate a generation of engineers who can harness AI's power while navigating its complexities.

While this contextualization is important, most AI education still occurs within computer science departments, creating a gap in providing engineering students the domain-specific AI competencies they will need in the workforce \cite{xxx}.
One issue with having engineering students learn AI in computer science courses is knowledge retention \cite{menekse2023envisioning}. 
Numerous engineering students have enrolled in computer science classes about data science and AI; however, in-major faculty have reported concerns about knowledge retention and translation [HDR 3,5].
Directly integrating AI education with field-specific expertise can offer recognized advantages [HDR 3,4]. However, there are many barriers to this, such as limited credit allowances within programs, making it difficult to accommodate new materials, and many current faculty lack training or expertise in AI, hindering their ability to teach the subjects [HDR 13]. 

Another concern is that computer science as a field has historically suffered from underrepresentation of certain racial and ethnic minority groups, women, and other marginalized populations \cite{aspray2016broadening, beyer2014women, scott2017broadening, charleston2014navigating}. 
Since AI education has been concentrated in these computer science programs, it inherently limits the exposure and participation of underrepresented students in this critical emerging area. 
As AI integrates across engineering disciplines, it is essential that educational opportunities are accessible from the start to a diverse range of students\cite{walsh2022aspiring}, not just those already in the computer science pipeline.

\subsection{Previous Existing NSF Project}
Previously funded NSF work by PI Napolitano, CoPI Reinhart, CoPI Wu, CoPI Moreu, CoPI Paal, and CoPI Solnosky work aimed to develop a curricular framework for data science education and workforce development that was grounded in community-centered data that could be integrated into existing STEM programs.
The key components included creating engineering-contextualized data science curricula and engaging community stakeholders, industry partners, and student organizations to provide real-world problems and flexible learning pathways.
While the previous project made strides in developing a curricular framework for integrating community-centered data science education into engineering classes, there remain crucial gaps that the current ``transformation" track proposal seeks to fill. 

Specifically in the previous work, the focus was solely on creating community-centered datasets and data science content, without intentional consideration of evidence-based pedagogy or strategies to broaden participation in computing through inclusive teaching practices. 
Dedicated diversity and inclusion consultants and assessors were not involved in facilitating intentional pedagogical choices.
Additionally, the developed curriculum focused primarily on positioning engineering students as \textbf{users} of data science techniques, lacking emphasis on developing them as \textbf{creators} who can build tailored data science models and applications, or as \textbf{problematizers} who critically examine the implications and limitations of data-driven systems.
Lastly, the scope of the previous work was limited to in-class activities and did not extend to creating structured pathways to guide students in translating their newfound data science skills into career opportunities aligned with their interests.
The current ``transformation" track proposal seeks to address these gaps by incorporating educational expertise, fostering inclusive learning environments, providing comprehensive career pathway support, and equipping students to be effective users, creators, and problematizers of data science and AI technologies within engineering contexts.

\section{Fundamental research questions and intellectual merit} 
The intellectual merit of this work lies in its comprehensive approach to addressing the critical gaps in AI education within engineering curricula, particularly for underrepresented groups and MSIs.  
By developing EMxAI educational resources, fostering communities of practice among professors at different institutions, and creating intentional career pathways, this project tackles the multifaceted challenges of integrating AI across engineering disciplines in an inclusive and equitable manner.
Central to this effort are the following research questions (RQs) that will guide the investigation and implementation of EMxAI education:
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=mycmykcolor, colframe=black, boxrule=1pt, coltext=black]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[] \textbf{RQ 1:} How can AI educational resources be effectively contextualized for specific EM to promote student learning and application of EMxAI concepts, while also addressing the needs and challenges of underrepresented groups in computing? 
    
    \item[] \textbf{RQ 2:}  What are the essential features and functionalities of an educational infrastructure that can effectively support the sharing of EMxAI resources across institutions, particularly those serving underrepresented groups in computing? 

    \item[] \textbf{RQ 3:}  To what extent do the developed EMxAI resources and educational infrastructure impact student learning outcomes, particularly for underrepresented groups in computing? 
    
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}

This project has the potential to advance multiple fields through its interdisciplinary approach to integrating AI education into EM curricula. 
By investigating how AI educational resources can be effectively contextualized for specific engineering mechanics domains, this research will contribute new insights into the unique pedagogical challenges and opportunities associated with integrating AI concepts into discipline-specific engineering curricula. 
The hands-on, problem-based approach to contextualizing AI within areas like structural analysis, manufacturing, and energy modeling will not only drive innovation in novel AI applications tailored to real-world engineering challenges but also inform the design of more targeted and effective AI learning materials and assessments across other STEM fields.
Additionally, the research on identifying the essential features and functionalities of educational infrastructure for sharing AI-integrated engineering mechanics resources across institutions, particularly those serving underrepresented groups in computing, will yield novel insights into the technological and social factors that facilitate collaborative learning and resource-sharing. 
These findings can guide the development of more inclusive and accessible platforms for disseminating interdisciplinary AI education resources and best practices.

Through its synergistic efforts across AI, engineering mechanics, and education research, this work will advance the field of engineering education by developing and evaluating translatable and scalable frameworks for effectively integrating interdisciplinary computing concepts into domain-specific curricula at a diverse range of institutions. 
The outcomes will inform best practices for creating equitable learning environments, fostering communities of practice, and designing intentional career pathways that broaden participation in emerging technologies for underrepresented groups.
Consequently, this project holds the promise of catalyzing advances across multiple spheres--shaping the future of inclusive engineering education, diversifying the technology workforce, and driving innovation at the intersection of AI and EM application areas.


\section{Research plan} The work is comprised of four tasks.
Task 1 directly addresses RQ 1 and 2; Task 2 develops the material necessary to examine RQ3 in Task 3; Task 4 ensures that the material is sustainable past the grant tenure.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=mycmykcolor, colframe=black, boxrule=1pt, coltext=black]

The initial knowledge base development will start by breaking the first RQ into subquestions, data to be gathered for answering each subquestion, assessment mechanisms, and outcomes. 
RQ1 was ``How can AI educational resources be effectively contextualized for specific EM
to promote student learning and application of EMxAI concepts, while also addressing
the needs and challenges of underrepresented groups in computing?"
To answer this research question, there are several subquestions that this can be broken down into:
\begin{itemize}

\item[] RQ 1.1. What are the key AI concepts and skills that are most relevant for EM, and how can they be made accessible and engaging for diverse student populations?
\item[] RQ 1.2. How can AI curricula be designed to align with the existing learning objectives and requirements of undergraduate engineering programs while promoting inclusivity and equity?
\end{itemize}


For subquestion 1.1, which focuses on identifying the key AI concepts and skills that are most relevant and essential for each engineering domain and how they can be made accessible and engaging for diverse student populations, we will gather data through Survey 1 (S1) with engineering students, faculty, and industry professionals based on xx existing surveys and protocols described below. 
S1 is a branching survey designed to gather input from different stakeholder groups on the key AI concepts and skills relevant to EM faculty, students, and workforce. 
The survey begins with a common set of questions that all participants answer, capturing general background information based on xxx.
It then branches into three distinct paths based on the participant's role/status.
Engineering faculty will receive a tailored set of questions based on xxx focused on identifying the core AI topics/skills needed in their specific engineering subdiscipline and ideas for contextualizing them effectively in the curriculum.
Students will receive a different tailored set of questions based on xxx focused on AI learning needs, interests, and accessibility barriers from the student perspective across different demographics.
The last branch will be for industry professionals and EM employers based on xxx to convey the AI competencies most valued in their future engineering workforce. 
After completing the respective branched sections, all participants will then reconvene and answer a common set of open-ended questions based on xxx. 
This final section allows all groups to provide additional insights, comments, or suggestions beyond the structured questions.
By employing this branching approach, S1 can capture nuanced perspectives from faculty leading EMxAI-integrated curricula, students experiencing it firsthand, and industry stakeholders influencing workforce needs, generating a comprehensive dataset to inform the project's objectives around contextualized AI education resources and pathways.
 
Additionally, we will conduct focus groups (FG1) with diverse engineering students to understand their learning needs, preferences, and challenges based on xxx existing protocols \cite{xxx}. 
The data collected will be analyzed using thematic analysis to identify common themes and patterns based on xxx and descriptive statistics will be used to summarize key findings and trends based on xxx. 
The synthesis of these results will lead to the development of a comprehensive list of key AI concepts and skills for EMxAI, as well as guidelines and recommendations for making AI learning materials accessible and engaging for diverse student populations. 
These concepts and skills will be shared with the Education Committee at the EMI annual conference for feedback and evaluation; subsequently those findings will be summarized into an initial roadmap for this project seeking to broaden participation in engineering-contextualized AI education.

Subquestion 1.2 aims to understand how AI curricula can be designed to align with the existing learning objectives and Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology (ABET) requirements of undergraduate engineering programs. 
To address this, we will conduct a curriculum mapping and alignment exercise across multiple institutions. First, we will gather extensive curriculum data, including course descriptions, learning outcomes, syllabi, and other materials from a diverse range of undergraduate engineering programs as well as existing AI and computing courses offered. Leveraging our team's expertise, including Co-PI Solnosky's experience as the ABET coordinator for Architectural Engineering at Penn State, we will map the identified AI concepts and skills against the ABET student learning outcomes and program criteria.
We will then perform curriculum alignment analyses, drawing upon established methods such as the Surveys of Enacted Curriculum (SEC) model \cite{blank2005surveys}, to identify gaps, redundancies, and opportunities for coherent integration of AI topics within the existing engineering curricula frameworks. 
In parallel, we will collect open-ended question data in S1 from engineering faculty across institutions to gain insights into the perceived barriers, challenges, and successful strategies for implementing AI-integrated curricula while continuing to meet ABET accreditation standards.

The synthesis of the curriculum mapping, alignment analyses, and qualitative faculty input will be triangulated to develop evidence-based guidelines and recommendations. These will provide concrete guidance on integrating AI competencies across courses and disciplines while satisfying ABET criteria through contextualized, applied AI learning experiences. The guidelines will emphasize the adoption of inclusive instructional design principles such as cultural responsiveness as guided by PSU's DDAR DEI program, universal design, and embedded pathways to broaden participation in computing for underrepresented student groups. 
Implementation strategies, sample curriculum models, tips for institutional adoption, and plans for assessing the new AI-integrated curricula against program requirements will also be included. 
The outcomes will enable engineering programs to fuse AI education with existing frameworks in an equitable, evidence-based manner.

After we go through these subquestions we can turn our knowledge base development towards RQ2: ``What are the essential features and functionalities of an educational infrastructure that can effectively support the sharing of AI resources across institutions, particularly those serving underrepresented groups in computing?"  To answer this research question, there are several subquestions that can be broken down into:
\begin{itemize}

\item[]RQ 2.1. How can a platform or repository be designed to facilitate easy access, contribution, and use of shared AI educational materials, ensuring that it is inclusive and accessible to educators and students from diverse backgrounds?
\item[]RQ 2.2. What standards and guidelines are needed to ensure the quality, relevance, and interoperability of shared resources?
\item[]RQ 2.3. How can the infrastructure foster collaboration and knowledge exchange among educators from different institutions, especially those serving underrepresented populations in computing?
\end{itemize}

For subquestion 2.1, which focuses on designing a platform or repository that facilitates easy access, contribution, and use of shared AI educational materials while ensuring inclusivity and accessibility for educators and students from diverse backgrounds, we will gather data from faculty through additional questions on S1 based on xx existing surveys and protocols \cite{xxx}.
This will help us understand their requirements, preferences, and challenges in accessing and using shared AI educational resources. 
The data collected will be analyzed using thematic analysis to identify common themes and patterns in user needs and preferences.  
The synthesis of these results will inform the development of an inclusive and accessible platform design, along with user guides and tutorials that cater to the needs of diverse users. 

Subquestion 2.2 aims to identify the standards and guidelines needed to ensure the quality, relevance, and interoperability of shared resources while promoting diversity, equity, and inclusion in AI education. To address this, we will gather data by reviewing existing educational resource standards and adding questions to S1 to understand faculty perspectives on the interoperability of AI educational resources. 
The synthesis of these results will lead to the development of a set of standards and guidelines for ensuring the quality, relevance, and interoperability of shared AI resources. 

Subquestion 2.3 focuses on how the infrastructure can foster collaboration and knowledge exchange among educators from different institutions, especially those serving underrepresented populations in computing. 
To address this, we will have questions on S1 to understand faculty needs, challenges, and preferences for collaboration and knowledge exchange. 
On S1, we will also collect data on the usage patterns and social interactions within existing collaboration platforms to identify effective strategies for fostering engagement and knowledge sharing. 
Content analysis will be used to identify common themes and best practices in collaborative knowledge exchange. 
The synthesis of these results will inform the development of features, tools, and community-building activities that promote collaboration and knowledge exchange among educators from diverse institutions.

\subsection{Intended population to be served}

The proposed project aims to serve a diverse population of undergraduate students pursuing engineering degrees across multiple institutions, with a particular focus on those from groups historically underrepresented in computing and AI fields, such as black and Hispanic students and first-generation college students. 
The intended population comprises undergraduate students enrolled in engineering programs at participating institutions, HBCU, HSIs, and FGFI who are currently underrepresented in computing and AI fields. 
The needs of this diverse population are multifaceted and include access to inclusive and engaging AI educational resources that are contextualized within their specific engineering domains, making the content more relevant and meaningful to their academic and career goals. 
Exposure to diverse role models and mentors in AI and computing fields, including faculty members and industry professionals from underrepresented groups, is crucial for fostering a sense of belonging and inspiration. 
Opportunities to engage in hands-on, project-based learning experiences that allow students to apply AI concepts and skills to real-world engineering problems are also important. 
Guidance and support in exploring career pathways and opportunities in AI and computing fields, including research experiences, and networking events, are critical for the intended population. 
Finally, culturally responsive teaching practices and inclusive pedagogical approaches that acknowledge and value the diverse backgrounds, experiences, and learning styles of underrepresented students are essential for creating an equitable and inclusive learning environment.  

By addressing the unique characteristics and needs of this diverse population of undergraduate engineering students, the proposed project aims to broaden participation in computing and AI education, ultimately contributing to a more diverse and inclusive workforce in these fields. The development of contextualized AI educational resources, collaborative communities of practice, and inclusive pedagogical strategies will help to create more equitable learning opportunities and support the academic and career success of underrepresented students in engineering and computing disciplines.

\subsection{Plans for accommodating intended populations}

To effectively address the needs of the diverse population of undergraduate engineering students, the proposed project will implement several specific plans and strategies. Firstly, the project will develop contextualized AI educational resources by collaborating with faculty members from various engineering disciplines to create AI learning modules, case studies, and projects that are directly relevant to each domain. Student representatives from underrepresented groups will be engaged in the design and review process to ensure that the resources are inclusive, accessible, and culturally responsive. Multiple modes of content delivery (e.g., text, video, audio) will be provided to accommodate different learning styles and preferences.

Secondly, the project will foster inclusive communities of practice by establishing networking programs that connect underrepresented students with faculty members and industry professionals from diverse backgrounds in AI and computing fields.  Seminars and networking events that highlight the contributions and perspectives of underrepresented groups in AI and computing will also be organized as discussed in Task 2.

Thirdly, the project will implement culturally responsive teaching practices by providing professional development opportunities for faculty members to learn about inclusive pedagogical approaches and culturally responsive teaching strategies facilitated through collaboration with the PSU DDAR DEI program.
Faculty will be encouraged to incorporate diverse perspectives, examples, and case studies in their EMxAI to reflect the experiences and interests of underrepresented students. Students' regular feedback will be sought and used to continuously improve and adapt teaching practices to better meet the needs of diverse learners.

Fourthly, the project will offer hands-on, project-based learning experiences by developing AI projects and assignments that allow students to apply their knowledge and skills to real-world engineering problems relevant to their communities and interests. Partnerships with industry and community organizations will be established to provide opportunities for students to work on authentic AI projects and gain practical experience. Student projects and achievements will be showcased through exhibitions, competitions, and symposia to celebrate their successes and inspire others.

Finally, the project will provide career guidance and support by collaborating with career services offices and industry partners to offer tailored career advice, job search assistance, and interview preparation for underrepresented students interested in the intersection of EM with AI and computing fields. Career fairs, workshops, and info sessions that connect students with employers and professionals from diverse backgrounds in AI and computing will be organized. Scholarships, internships, and research opportunities specifically targeted at underrepresented students will be established to support their career exploration and advancement in AI fields.

By implementing these specific plans and strategies, the proposed project aims to create an inclusive, supportive, and engaging learning environment that accommodates the diverse needs of underrepresented students in undergraduate engineering programs. These efforts will help to remove barriers, provide equitable access to AI education, and ultimately contribute to a more diverse and inclusive workforce in AI and computing fields.
