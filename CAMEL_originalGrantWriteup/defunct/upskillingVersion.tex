\section{Introduction}

This transformation proposal addresses the key challenge of effectively integrating artificial intelligence (AI) into undergraduate engineering curricula to prepare our graduates for success in the future AI-driven engineering workforce.

Presently, there is a growing demand being placed on CISE departments to teach this material to students from all disciplines. 
The proposed work will ease that burden by facilitating the teaching of AI topics in engineering-domain-specific courses. 

To do this, the proposed work seeks to develop: 

* engineering-contextualized AI educational resources such as curricula, data sources, and assessments

* educational infrastructure to support sharing such materials across institutions

* and communities of engineering professors trying to upskill in AI topics (groups, workshops, train the trainer, etc)

To ensure that this work supports students form groups historically underrepresented in computing, the project will be a collaborative effort across multiple universities with diverse faculty participation.
The project team includes a female assistant professor at Penn State, a female associate professor at Texas A\&M University, a Hispanic-serving institution, a female, Asian assistant professor at Tennessee State University, a historically Black college, and a male, Latino associate professor at University of New Mexico, a Hispanic-serving institution. 
In terms of specific engineering, the PI and CoPIs range across several subdomains. PI Napolitano's research and undergraduate teaching focus on historic preservation and damage diagnostics; CoPI Paal's research and undergraduate teaching focus on risk assessment and modeling structural stability; CoPI Reinhart's research and undergraduate teaching focus on optimized design and control of additive manufacturing processes; CoPI Wang's research and undergraduate teaching focus on building energy modeling; CoPI Moreu's research and undergraduate teaching focus on structural health monitoring.

\subsection{Barriers to AI Education Integration for Non-CS Faculty}

The integration of AI content into undergraduate engineering programs by non-CS professors is crucial, given the significant shortage of computer science faculty; in 2021, 53\% of computer science, computer engineering, and information faculty positions remained unfilled \cite{MaslejEtAl2023}. Consequently, engineering faculty members are increasingly needed to teach AI-related skills, despite facing significant barriers.

As highlighted by \cite{griffith2020framework}, a faculty member's workload ($W$) comprises three main components: teaching ($T$), service ($S$), and research ($R$), which can be represented by the equation:

\begin{equation}
    T + S + R = W
\end{equation}

However, the time devoted to teaching extends beyond merely the hours spent in the classroom. It encompasses the time spent physically teaching the course ($T_C$), the administrative work related to teaching ($T_A$), and the total preparation time required ($T_P$). Furthermore, $T_A$ should account for the number of semester credits taught and be multiplied by a factor representing the number of students in each class. Therefore, the equation becomes:

\begin{equation}
    T_C + T_A + T_P + R + S = W
\end{equation}

AI and ML courses are highly popular and tend to fill up quickly, potentially increasing the value of $T_A$ compared to traditional domain courses. Moreover, the rapid evolution of the AI field necessitates continuous knowledge updates for instructors, significantly increasing the preparation time ($T_P$). Consequently, the equation can be modified as follows:

\begin{equation}
    T_C + T_A + \alpha T_P + R + S = W
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ is a factor representing the additional preparation time required for AI and ML courses.

However, this equation does not account for the time needed for upskilling ($T_U$), which is particularly relevant when faculty members lack prior knowledge or formal training in AI and ML. In such cases, they must allocate additional time for upskilling themselves in these domains, leading to the following equation:

\begin{equation}
    T_C + T_A + \alpha T_P + T_U + R + S = W
\end{equation}

Addressing these barriers to learning new material, creating new courses, and teaching AI and ML content requires institutional support and dedicated resources, such as professional development opportunities, course release time, and access to relevant educational materials and expertise.

Failure to mitigate these challenges could result in suboptimal learning experiences for students, as faculty members may struggle to keep up with the latest developments in AI while balancing their existing teaching and research responsibilities. Assuming a fixed number of working hours ($W$), they are forced to reduce their research ($R$), service ($S$), or preparation time ($T_P$) to accommodate the additional time required for upskilling and teaching AI and ML courses.

Furthermore, the challenges faced by minority faculty members (MFM) and those teaching at MSIs are compounded. Numerous publications have documented higher service loads for diverse faculty and faculty at diverse institutions \cite{hanasono2019secre, o2017asked}. This can be represented by introducing a factor $\beta$ to account for the increased service load:

\begin{equation}
    T_C + T_A + \alpha T_P + T_U + R + \beta S = W
\end{equation}

Evaluations of teaching experiences at MSIs have also found that professors often need to dedicate more hours to student learning \cite{conrad2015educating} due to systemic inequalities perpetuated in primary education. This can be represented by introducing a factor $\gamma$ to account for the increased administrative work related to teaching:

\begin{equation}
    T_C + \gamma T_A + \alpha T_P + T_U + R + \beta S = W
\end{equation}


So, if we were to compare the workloads of a non-MFM at a non-MSI institution with a MFM at an MSI that would be: 

\begin{equation}
    \Delta W = (1-\gamma)T_A + (1-\alpha) T_P + (1-\beta) S
\end{equation}

Using this equation, we can assume that a nonMFM/MSI faculty member spends 5 hours per week on teaching activities ($T_A$), preparation ($T_P$), and service ($S$). Furthermore, we can assume that an MFM at an MSI spends 30\% more time on these activities compared to a nonMFM/MSI faculty member. Based on these assumptions, the difference in the amount of extra time spent on $T_A$, $T_P$, and $S$ between an MFM at an MSI and a nonMFM/MSI faculty member is 10.5 hours.
And let's look at this another way. 
Assume that both faculty members can spend a maximum of 40 hours per week on work, and both spend 5 hours per week on $T_C$ $T_U$. 
Given these assumptions, the nonMFM/MSI faculty member can spend 20 hours per week on research (40 hours - 5 hours for $T_A$, $T_P$, and $S$ - 5 hours for $T_C$ and $T_U$ = 20 hours for research). However, the MFM at an MSI can only spend 9.5 hours per week on research (40 hours - 15.5 hours for $T_A$, $T_P$, and $S$ - 5 hours for $T_C$ and $T_U$ = 9.5 hours for research).
Considering these compounding challenges, it becomes evident that asking minority faculty members and those at MSIs to integrate AI and ML education into their curricula may be an unrealistic expectation without substantial institutional support and dedicated resources to alleviate their existing workloads.


As mentioned above, PI Napolitano already led a successful NSF project where faculty were asked to include data science topics in their engineering courses. 
During this experience the friction between the time for teaching activities and the time for research activities became apparent. Semi-structured interviews with faculty members included the following responses: 

\begin{quote}
``As someone without a strong background in computer science or statistics, a lot of the data science concepts were completely new to me. I spent a lot of time watching online tutorials just to grasp the fundamentals. Balancing this self-learning with my regular teaching and research duties was incredibly demanding on my time and mental bandwidth."

``As a junior faculty member, I was already stretched thin. Learning data science felt like an added burden that I had to take on during evenings and weekends. I remember countless nights staying up late, poring over coding tutorials. It was a steep learning curve, and I often felt isolated."

\end{quote}


Based on the anecdotes from faculty members and the challenges they faced in upskilling to incorporate data science concepts into their curricula, it became evident that curriculum development alone was not sufficient. 
Many faculty members, particularly those from underrepresented groups or at minority-serving institutions, struggled to find the time and mental bandwidth required to learn and master the necessary data science techniques. The demands of balancing teaching, research, and service commitments, coupled with the steep learning curve and lack of support structures, created significant barriers to successful curriculum integration.
It became clear that in addition to developing new curricula, a comprehensive approach was required to equip faculty with the resources, training, and support needed for effective upskilling. This should include providing access to professional development opportunities, such as workshops, online tutorials, and mentorship programs, specifically tailored to the unique needs and challenges faced by faculty members from diverse backgrounds.
Furthermore, the project highlighted the importance of empowering faculty to effectively communicate the value and benefits of integrating data science and now AI concepts into their curricula during annual evaluations and tenure/promotion processes. Many faculty members expressed concerns about the potential negative impacts of dedicating significant time and effort towards curriculum development and upskilling on their research productivity and career advancement prospects.
To address these concerns, resources and guidance are needed to help faculty articulate the significance of these initiatives, demonstrate their alignment with institutional goals, and highlight the potential positive impacts on student learning outcomes and workforce preparation. By equipping faculty with the tools to effectively convey the benefits during annual evaluations and tenure/promotion processes, we can better advocate for the recognition and support needed to pursue these curricular innovations.

\subsection{Best Practices in Faculty Professional Development and Upskilling To Broadening Participation}

There has been a good deal of research done on best practices for faculty upskilling considering the rapid need for remote teaching and learning skills in the COVID-19 pandemic \cite{zeivots2021hybrid, laloo2020teaching, calonge2022contactless}.
\cite{macphail2019professional} examined the professional development of higher-education based teacher educators through semi-structured interviews and surveys and saw the gap between wanting to upskill and not having the time or institutional support \cite{macphail2019professional}. 
There are several findings from \cite{macphail2019professional} which will be used as a framework to build this proposed work off of.

A first item of note is that this study found evidence of a need for specific professional development materials. 
"One teacher educator commented on the more generic audiences for professional learning opportunities, at the expense of a dearth of professional learning opportunities for teacher educators, ‘(…) my level of satisfaction wouldn’t be as high with the generic opportunities that are afforded to academics generally’" \cite{macphail2019professional}. 

A related outcome of this work was that specific peer-learning communities across institutions are a preferred way to increase teacher educators' skills and knowledge \cite{macphail2019professional} as there can be a lack of necessary topical expertise within one institution or there could be issues of competition.

\section{Fundamental research questions and intellectual merit} 
The intellectual merit of this proposal lies in its approach to addressing the barriers and challenges faced by non-CS faculty, particularly those from underrepresented groups and minority-serving institutions, in integrating AI education into their undergraduate engineering curricula. By examining the most effective strategies for upskilling faculty members and establishing interdisciplinary learning communities, this study contributes to the growing body of knowledge on best practices for AI education integration in diverse institutional settings.
Furthermore, by investigating these issues and developing strategies to empower faculty in navigating their academic environments, this study adds to the understanding of the systemic barriers that hinder the advancement of AI education in underrepresented communities.
The research questions posed in this proposal are grounded in the identified gaps in existing literature and the lessons learned from previous NSF projects on data science education. By addressing the following questions, the study aims to generate new insights and practical recommendations that can inform future efforts to broaden participation and promote equitable access to AI education in undergraduate engineering programs.

    \item How can minority faculty and faculty at MSIs effectively advocate for the integration of AI education into their curricula, secure institutional support and resources, and navigate potential challenges within their academic environments?

By rigorously investigating these questions, the project has the potential to yield valuable insights and practical strategies for promoting inclusive AI education across various engineering and scientific disciplines. Addressing the professional development needs of faculty, especially those from underrepresented backgrounds, is a crucial step towards democratizing access to cutting-edge AI knowledge and skills. 

The findings and insights gained from this study will be highly translatable to other educational contexts and disciplines beyond undergraduate engineering programs studied. The strategies developed for upskilling faculty members, particularly those from underrepresented groups and minority-serving institutions, can serve as a model for other STEM fields looking to integrate emerging technologies into their undergraduate curricula. The best practices identified for establishing interdisciplinary learning communities and professional networks can be adapted and implemented in various academic settings, fostering collaboration and knowledge sharing across institutions and disciplines.
Moreover, the lessons learned from empowering minority faculty and those at MSIs to advocate for the integration of AI education and navigate institutional challenges will have broad implications for promoting diversity, equity, and inclusion in higher education. The strategies and recommendations developed through this study can inform efforts to support and empower underrepresented faculty members in other disciplines and institutional contexts, helping to dismantle systemic barriers and create more inclusive academic environments.
By documenting and disseminating the findings, tools, and resources developed through this project, we aim to create a ripple effect that extends beyond the immediate scope of the study;  other institutions, departments, and faculty members can leverage these resources to inform their own efforts to integrate AI education and support underrepresented groups in their respective fields. This translational potential amplifies the impact of the project, contributing to the broader goal of preparing a diverse workforce with the AI competencies needed to drive innovation and address complex societal challenges.

\subsection{Expected significance}: 
By developing effective strategies for upskilling faculty members and establishing interdisciplinary learning communities, this study advances the theoretical understanding of how to successfully integrate AI education into undergraduate curricula, particularly in diverse institutional settings. The research findings will also contribute to the development of innovative methodologies for faculty professional development, curriculum design, and assessment of AI competencies in engineering education. The expected outcomes of this research include the advancement of theory and methodology related to AI education integration, faculty upskilling, and promoting diversity and inclusion in STEM fields. The insights gained from this study will inform the development of evidence-based policies, guidelines, and practices for supporting faculty members, particularly those from underrepresented groups and minority-serving institutions, in integrating AI education into their curricula. These practical implications will help institutions and departments create more inclusive and equitable learning environments, foster interdisciplinary collaboration, and prepare a diverse student population for success in the AI-driven workforce. In the long term, this research has the potential to catalyze systemic change in higher education, breaking down barriers and promoting equitable access to AI education for underrepresented students in engineering and beyond. By empowering minority faculty and those at MSIs to advocate for AI education integration and secure institutional support, this study contributes to the larger goal of diversifying the AI workforce and ensuring that the benefits of AI technologies are accessible to all communities. The translatable strategies and resources developed through this project will have a lasting impact, enabling institutions across the nation to effectively integrate AI education and support underrepresented groups in STEM fields, ultimately driving innovation, economic growth, and societal progress.
